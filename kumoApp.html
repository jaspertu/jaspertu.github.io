    Kumo Companion App

    Track Kumo usage and stay aware of your surroundings

    Jasper Tu • 10 min read

    Background

    In 2020, COVID-19 ravaged the world and mask-wearing became a global norm. I was part of an ambitious zero-to-one project to design a speculative smart device and companion app speaking to this health crisis and future infectious incidents. The Kumo app fosters a safe breathing experience for all, especially those with respiratory challenges—by keeping users aware of their breathing and environment using the Kumo mask.

    My Contribution

    As the product design lead, I helped steer our product strategy and design thinking journey, mentoring the other designers along the way. Doubling as a product designer, I also conducted research, wireframing, prototyping, user testing, and spearheaded the development of the design system.

    Contextual Recap

    Revisiting the People Problem

    Interviews and online research revealed current face mask options were uncomfortable and unsafe for those with respiratory conditions.

    Masks like the N95 trap hot air and make breathing difficult, which can trigger respiratory flare-ups. Others like cloth and surgical masks offer unreliable filtration protection against smaller airborne molecules.

    The Process

    Using the Double Diamond

    While we now had an understanding of the people problem and core functions of the Kumo mask, we didn't have a granular picture yet of what the app would do. All we knew was the smart mask could track breathing data and relay it to the app. Working off this knowledge, we used the Double Diamond again to guide our thinking and pinpoint a HMW for the app.

    Discover

    Research Never Stops

    Making data-informed decisions key and having more data helps generate a more holistic understanding of a problem space. As the product design lead, I took initiative to continue primary research to gather more insights, by creating a survey and circulating it to respiratory patients and friends/family of said patients. Context was given about our Kumo smart mask and its capabilities, and questions were centered on discovering what users might find helpful to have in the app. Here were the key points:

    76% were interested in learning about local environmental factors to avoid harmful locations or prepare for them
    80% wanted real-time updates on their breathing status
    90% of parents with kids who had respiratory conditions wanted to be able to monitor their kid's wellbeing

    Flow and Usability

    Interaction Design Decisions

    Supporting Multiple Masks

    "I need at least 6 masks to last me through the whole week of work."
    —Fulltime Uniqlo sales associate

    Assumption: Most people own multiple masks for convenience—I'm a part-timer at Uniqlo with 3 masks. I realized this meant users needed an efficient way to manage their masks. Beyond adding a new or existing mask, how would a first-time user name their mask? How would a user rename an existing mask? How would a user remove an existing mask?

    Result: I prototyped 2 flows—one without Edit Masks (rename, delete) in Devices and one with Edit Masks functionality. I tested both concepts with fellow fulltimers who worked at least 5 days/week and successfully verified my hypothesis.

    Action: I added mask naming to the Onboarding flow. I also added an Edit Masks button to the Devices screen—3 versions were made and A/B tested. Option A was chosen because the Edit Masks button would never conflict with the Add Mask button if there were more masks extending past the scroll.

    Rethinking Maps—Eng Constraints

    "There are currently no databases or APIs which track geographically for pollen, dust, mold."
    —Software engineer

    Before: We had the idea of a Map feature that could map out pollen, dust, mould levels geographically so users would know to avoid/prepare entry into such locations.

    SWE Consultation: However, I talked to a SWE friend and discovered databases don't exist which track these attributes.

    After: The Map feature was scrapped. Since COVID-19 location tracking was the only viable dataset, this was implemented into the COVID-19 Exposure card which can be added onto the Home screen.

    Managing Accounts—Thinking of Edge Cases

    Problem: How might users efficiently switch accounts? How might users efficiently manage added accounts of various quantities? If a user had 6 accounts added to their app, I realized it isn't practical for all 6 accounts to only be able to stack above each other. From a user POV, it's unnecessary to show all accounts at the same time.

    Solution: I implemented a Manage Accounts feature into the slide-up menu. In Manage Accounts, existing profiles can be hidden (by toggling off) or removed entirely.

    Developing & Refactoring UI

    Visual Design Decisions

    To foster efficiency and consistency with reusable components in hi-fi, I spearheaded the development of a design system with type, colours, and interaction components.

    Typography—Form Meets Function

    Söhne was chosen because unlike the angular geometries of popular grotesque typefaces, it invites a sense of softness without sacrificing legibility. This aspect speaks to the friendly nature of our product and brand, without sacrificing modernity. Metric was chosen for body because its smooth geometric forms match well with the softness of Söhne. It also boasts great readability at smaller sizes, ideal for body copy. Both typefaces have good x-height.

    An Accessible Colour System

    Keeping in tune with the Friendly brand principle, bright colours were implemented to add a sense of optimism to the app. A colour system based on clear, vibrant hues also makes it easy for users to differentiate between colours. Each hue is optimized for predictable accessibility, with contrast ratios never going below 3.0.

    Refactoring COVID-19 Exposure

    Building on my teammate's initial design, I spotted opportunities to use Progressive Disclosure and Hick's Law to focus the user's locus of attention. I asked myself: Do all case numbers across Daily, Weekly, Monthly need to be showed at once? How might we more clearly show that 'View Next Steps' button belongs under 'Exposure Log'? How do users access past exposure logs?

    Solution: I moved 'View Next Steps' button into the 'Particle Detected' card and added a secondary CTA for 'Past Exposures'. COVID-19 case numbers were simplified to Cases, Fatalities, and Recoveries across a 'Daily', 'Weekly', 'Monthly' view. An 'Overall' section was added to the tabs to show total numbers since the pandemic began.

    Clarity and Hierarchy in Home

    What does the number representing Air Quality Index actually mean? How do I know when the air is clean or not based on this number?
    —Student I tested with

    When I took Kumo out for user testing, someone said they didn't understand what the Air Quality Index actually meant. What does an AQI of 19 entail? What about 33? Diving deeper into a user's mental model, I realized the typical person wouldn't know this so more clarity was needed to attach meaning to these AQI values.

    Solution: After consulting with my team and reviewing research, I decided to revamp the layout and hierarchically emphasize AQI as it was the most important out of Temperature and Humidity. This also made room for a label to denote meaning of the AQI value on a scale of Poor, Moderate, Good.
