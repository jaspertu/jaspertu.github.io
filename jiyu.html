    Jiyu

    Enabling expression for all with virtual fitting

    Jasper Tu • 5 min read

    Background

    This 0-to-1 project was done for my undergraduate thesis, created from an iterative product design process involving real users. The Jiyu mobile app offers a new way to shop online through a virtual fitting room experience that uses a custom avatar of the shopper. It houses a suite of fun and simple tools to help shoppers determine the right fashion items and size for them, so they can express themselves fully and discover their unique style.

    Defining the people problem

    Fitting rooms are the most critical point in the shopper journey because it’s where a purchase decision is made; shoppers who use a fitting room are 7x more likely to convert.
    And yet, only 1 in 10 shoppers are satisfied with the current brick-and-mortar fitting room experience. Why is this so?

    Pain point 1

    Messy, cramped, poorly lit rooms fail to let shoppers be comfortably vulnerable.

    Pain point 2

    Long queues turn off shoppers from trying an item on.

    Pain point 3

    Being unable to find an item/size prevents shoppers from reaching a purchase decision.

    Pain point 4

    Item limits when trying on slow shoppers from experimenting with outfit combinations.

    Pain point 5

    Shoppers are limited to trying on items that belong to a specific store.

    Furthermore, online shopping doesn’t allow shoppers to experience an item physically, which leads many to buy multiples to try—then go through the pain of returning those that don’t work.

    The 2nd dimension—a Gen Z expression problem

    Adjacent to discovering the need for an improved fitting room experience was the discovery that 73% of Gen Z (born 1997–2012) desire greater virtual self-expression to lead a happier, healthier life.

    This is relevant because these digital natives not only represent the present and future generation of shoppers, but reflect a growing ideal—the notion that identity extends beyond the physical because the online world is where friends are primarily met and personalities are expressed.

    Understanding the user frustrations, solutions were hypothesized around:

    How might we equip the shoppers of today & tomorrow with a fitting room experience that enhances creativity & makes it easier to arrive at a purchase decision?

    Getting Fresh Eyes

    User testing

    Once I had mid-fidelity screens and user flows established, concept testing was conducted with 2 participants to get a pulse for the ideal visual directions to head in, and amplify understanding of technical constraints to make key design decisions on how the system would function if this were built IRL. Key insights:

    • Avatar creation
    • PDP
    • Search filter

    Conducting a body scan with phone camera to create an accurate avatar of the shopper was deemed unrealistic given current technological constraints. Thus, manual input of body measurements was used, with illustrations teaching the user how to measure their body.

    There was an overall preference for housing product info in a sticky and extendable bottom sheet to place visual focus on the product image. Progressive disclosure groups and prioritizes the most important info (brand, size + color selection, price, etc.), while giving access to other info and functions when sliding the bottom sheet up to extend it (style selection, reviews, etc.).

    There was an overall preference for housing all controls together in one page so shoppers could easily adjust multiple settings in one instance, as shoppers can get quite specific in their search preferences.

    Forming an appropriate brand

    The logo of Jiyu takes after the form of the sparkle. The sparkle is tied to positive sentiments, including love, happiness, beauty, gratitude, and excitement. This fits with Jiyu’s mission to be a vessel for personal expression and influence through fashion, where everyone is encouraged to feel comfortable and positive about trying new items and expressing who they are. To elevate brand presence, the logo is used as the central bottom nav button and pulls double duty with its resemblance to the “+” (connoting addition of items to the avatar). Sparkles are also used as subtle animations post-dressup, to communicate the outfit has been updated.

    Introducing the solution, Jiyu

    Jiyu is a mobile app that provides modular fitting of fashion items by creating an avatar of the shopper that's accurate to their body proportions. The video below walks through the core flows and functions:

    Avatar creation • Dress up • Scale + rotate avatar • Style options • Feel fabric (Home + PDP) • Like an outfit + garment • Multi-brand carts + automatic coupons • Social feed • Smart suggestions • Re-ordering of layers

    Learnings

    Novelty is a game of remix

    I initially planned for an avatar creator concept with an integrated NFT marketplace but struggled to differentiate from competitors, as the creative tooling approach would either be too similar to products like Memoji or Maya. Hitting this dead-end ultimately prompted me to fuse the idea of an avatar creator with a virtual fitting room to define a new online shopping experience.

    Business strategy, Web 3 & the creator class

    In thinking about translating Jiyu into a viable business model, I was encouraged to think about how other brands could benefit from the proliferation of a virtual fitting room and brand agnostic concept like Jiyu. As shown in the social Feed, Jiyu presents huge opportunities for brands to release limited edition or temporary NFT drops that shoppers can try with their avatar, where certain items or collections can be promoted depending on a brand’s business goals and what is popular among the people. Celebrities can further propagate and move the needle on certain looks, brands, and items when they post to the Feed and influence the masses.
